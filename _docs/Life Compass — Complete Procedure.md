# Life Compass — Complete Procedure

> A step-by-step guide to filling in, maintaining, and using the Life Compass. Every quest, every weekly review, and every values check connects back to this document. If the Life Compass is weak, everything downstream is weaker.

**Location:** [[01-Vision/life-compass|01-Vision/life-compass.md]]
**Review frequency:** Every quarterly review (every 90 days) · Update immediately when something genuinely changes

---

## What the Life Compass is

The Life Compass is your personal operating system's foundation layer. It answers three questions:

1. **What do I actually value?** — Not what you think you should value. What genuinely matters to you when you're honest.
2. **Who am I trying to be in each role?** — Parent, professional, builder. What does showing up well actually look like?
3. **How is my life going right now?** — The energy audit gives each area a number so you can track direction over time.

Everything else in the vault uses this:
- Quests must connect to a value or role in the Life Compass
- Weekly reviews rate each life area using the same 6 categories
- Monthly and quarterly reviews check whether you lived your values
- The Future Vision describes where these values take you

If the Life Compass is blank or generic, the reviews become meaningless exercises. If it's specific and honest, every review becomes a genuine reckoning.

---

## Part 1: Core values

### What they are

Your core values are the 3–5 things that, when you violate them, make you feel like you've betrayed yourself. Not aspirations — not things you wish were true. Things that already feel like *you* when you're at your best.

The vault has five pre-filled values based on your Future Vision: Presence, Integrity, Creation, Foundation, Deliberateness. These are correct starting points — edit them to fit if needed, add or remove as your understanding evolves.

---

### Step 1: Write your personal definition

**Field:** "What it means to me"

This is not a dictionary definition. This is what the value means *in your life, in your context, in concrete situations.*

**How to do it:**
1. Open `01-Vision/life-compass.md` (press `Ctrl+H` or click Life Compass on the dashboard)
2. For each value, click inside the `>` blockquote after "What it means to me:"
3. Write 2–4 sentences in plain language. Be specific about the contexts where this value matters to you.

**Example of what NOT to write:** "Integrity means being honest."
**Example of what to write:** "Integrity means doing what I said I'd do. Being honest and straight with people — not managing impressions, not softening things to the point of misleading. The career I want is built on doing great work and being someone people trust."

**The test:** Could a stranger reading your definition understand exactly what you mean? Could they recognize a violation of it when they saw one?

---

### Step 2: Describe what violating it looks like

**Field:** "What violating it looks like"

This is the most important field in the values section. Violations are concrete and observable — they ground the value in reality.

**How to do it:**
1. Think of a time you felt bad about yourself. What value did that behavior violate?
2. Describe the specific behavior, not the feeling. "Saying yes when I mean maybe" is better than "not being honest enough."
3. Keep it to 2–3 sentences — specific, behavioral, observable.

**Why this matters:** When you do the weekly review and ask "did I live this value?", you need concrete behaviors to check against. Abstract violations ("I wasn't very present") don't change anything. Specific ones ("I was on my phone while my kids were talking to me") do.

---

### Step 3: Write how you want to show up

**Field:** "How I want to show up"

This is your aspiration — the best version of yourself expressing this value. Write it in the present tense as if it's already true.

**How to do it:**
1. Ask: if I were living this value fully, what would someone observing me notice?
2. Write 1–2 sentences. Keep it concrete enough to recognize.

**Example:** "Curious and strong-minded" (Presence). "Comfortable that I do not have to remember any lies — people can depend on what I tell them" (Integrity).

**Important:** Don't write a generic aspiration. "I want to be more present" is useless. "When I'm with my kids, the phone goes in another room and I give them my full attention" is actionable.

## Part 2: Life roles

### What they are

Roles are the hats you wear. Where values are internal (what you believe), roles are relational (how you show up for others). The three pre-defined roles are Parent, Professional, and Builder/Creator — add others as needed (partner, friend, community member, etc.).

---

### Step 4: Define "showing up well"

**Field:** "Showing up well looks like:"

This defines success in each role. It should be specific enough that at the end of any week, you know whether you did it or not.

**How to do it:**
1. Ask: if I were doing this role as well as I possibly could, what would that look like in practice?
2. Think of one person you know who shows up well in this role. What do they actually *do*?
3. Write 2–4 sentences. Behaviors, not feelings.

**Example:** "Present and engaged, not just in the same room. Full attention when we're together — the mental noise quieted enough to give them what they need."

---

### Step 5: Honestly identify where you're falling short

**Field:** "Where I'm falling short right now:"

This is the hardest section. It requires actual honesty, not polished self-awareness. Write the real thing, not the version that makes you look thoughtful.

**How to do it:**
1. Think about the last two weeks. In this role, what did you not do that you said you would?
2. What feedback have you received (directly or indirectly) that you're not meeting the standard?
3. Write 1–3 sentences. Plain language. No hedging.

**Example of hedging (don't write this):** "I could be more intentional about quality time."
**Example of honest (write this):** "Too busy with my own growth — spending more time on the vault than with the kids."

This section hurts to write. Write it anyway. The weekly review will reference it.

---

### Step 6: Commit to one change

**Field:** "One thing I want to do differently:"

One specific, behavioral change. Not a resolution — a commitment to a specific action.

**How to do it:**
1. Look at what you wrote in "falling short"
2. Ask: what is the single most concrete change that would address this?
3. Write one sentence. If it takes more than one sentence, it's too vague or too big.

**Example:** "Spend more time with the kids" — works. "Be more present in general" — doesn't work (too vague). "Put phone in another room every evening after dinner and spend 30 minutes with the kids doing whatever they want" — best (specific and testable).

## Part 3: Energy audit

### What it is

The energy audit rates your life across 6 areas on a 1–5 scale. The ratings aren't meant to be permanent — they're meant to reflect *right now* so you can see direction of travel at each review.

The six areas are:
- **Work & career** — quality of work, energy, progress, feeling of direction
- **Health & energy** — sleep, exercise, food, physical energy levels
- **Relationships** — connection, investment, quality of interactions with the people you care about
- **Personal growth** — learning, progress toward becoming who you want to be
- **Fun & creativity** — joy, play, creative expression, things you do just because you want to
- **Money & finances** — security, progress, absence of money stress

---

### Step 7: Rate each area honestly

**How to do it:**
1. Open the Energy Audit table in Life Compass
2. For each area, ask: "On a scale of 1–5, how is this area actually going *right now*?" Not how it should be. Not how it could be. How it IS.
3. Scoring guide:
   - **1** — This area is in crisis or completely neglected
   - **2** — Significantly below where I want it to be, problems are real
   - **3** — Functional but not thriving — acceptable, not good
   - **4** — Going well, mostly satisfied, minor gaps
   - **5** — Genuinely excellent, couldn't want much more right now
4. Fill in the "One honest sentence" column with a factual observation, not a judgment. "Just started a new job with Argenta" is better than "Struggling professionally."

**Common mistake:** Inflating ratings to feel better. The audit is only useful if it's honest. A 2 that you acknowledge is more useful than a 4 that avoids the real situation.

---

### Step 8: Update the last_reviewed date

After completing or reviewing the Life Compass:
1. Open the Properties panel (`⊞` icon at the top of the note)
2. Find `last_reviewed`
3. Enter today's date in `YYYY-MM-DD` format

This timestamp appears in quarterly reviews and tells you when the last honest look happened.

## Part 4: Quarterly review process

The Life Compass should be reviewed and potentially updated at every quarterly review (approximately every 90 days). Here's how:

---

### Step 9: Read it before reviewing

Before the quarterly review session, read the entire Life Compass from top to bottom. Don't edit while reading — just take it in. Ask yourself:
- Does this still feel true?
- Has anything shifted in how I think about these values?
- Have any of the roles changed?

---

### Step 10: Check the values section

For each value:
- Read your "What it means to me" — is it still accurate? Did you learn something this quarter that changes it?
- Read "What violating it looks like" — did you actually experience any of these violations? Was the description accurate?
- Read "How I want to show up" — are you closer to this than you were 90 days ago?

**Edit if:** Something genuinely shifted in your understanding. Not because you failed to live it, but because you see it differently.

**Don't edit if:** You just fell short of it. Falling short means keep working at it, not redefine it.

---

### Step 11: Update the roles section

The roles section changes more often than the values section as life circumstances shift.

For each role:
1. Update "Where I'm falling short right now" — what's true NOW, not what was true 90 days ago
2. Update "One thing I want to do differently" — should be a fresh commitment, not the same one from last quarter (if it's the same one, ask why it didn't happen)
3. Optionally: add a new role if your life has added a significant relationship or commitment

---

### Step 12: Re-do the energy audit

1. Rate all 6 areas fresh without looking at last quarter's numbers
2. After rating, compare to last quarter
3. Note the direction of travel (↑ ↑ → ↓) for each area
4. The areas that dropped are the candidates for next quarter's quest

**The key question:** "Which area, if I invested in it this quarter, would raise my overall satisfaction the most?" That area usually becomes a quest.

---

### Step 13: Update last_reviewed

Change `last_reviewed` in the Properties panel to today's date.

## Part 5: How the Life Compass connects to everything else

### Quests

Every quest must connect to the Life Compass. The quest template asks: "One sentence connecting to your Life Compass or Future Vision." If you can't write that sentence, the quest is being driven by obligation or external pressure, not your actual values.

Current quests:
- **Health Reset** → Foundation value + Health & energy audit area
- **New Role Integration** → Integrity + Deliberateness values + Work & career audit area

### Weekly reviews

Section 6 of the weekly review asks: "Did you act in line with your core values?" You open the Life Compass (or remember it), and answer honestly. The "violating it looks like" descriptions are what you check against.

Section 2 rates the same 6 life areas you rated in the energy audit, but weekly. Over time you can see whether your interventions (quests, habits) are actually moving the numbers.

### Monthly reviews

Section 7 does a formal values check — for each value, "How I lived it" and "Where I fell short." The Life Compass is the answer key.

### Quarterly reviews

The quarterly review includes a full Life Compass update (Steps 9–13 above) and a decision about whether the values and roles still reflect who you are.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Writing aspirations instead of truths**
The Life Compass should describe who you ARE and what you VALUE — not who you want to be. Values in the future tense are goals, not values. Keep it present tense and honest.

**Mistake 2: Keeping it at a high level**
"I value honesty" is not useful. "I value doing what I said I'd do, even when it's uncomfortable, and I feel genuinely bad when I manage someone's impression of me instead of being straight" is useful. The specificity is the point.

**Mistake 3: Not revisiting it**
A Life Compass filled in once and never touched again becomes a historical artifact rather than a living document. The quarterly review is the minimum cadence for a check-in.

**Mistake 4: Treating it as performance**
This document is private and functional. Write what's true, not what sounds good. The "falling short" sections are the most valuable fields in the document — they only work if they're honest.

**Mistake 5: Making quests without consulting it**
If a quest doesn't connect to the Life Compass, it's responding to external pressure. Use the compass to sanity-check every new commitment.
