## Key ideas (in your own words)

1. The mind is for having ideas, not storing them. Every open loop — an unresolved commitment, an undone task, an unprocessed thought — takes up mental RAM. The entire GTD system exists to get these out of your head and into a trusted external system so your brain can focus on doing instead of remembering.

2. Your ability to generate power is directly proportional to your ability to relax. A completely processed inbox creates genuine mental calm, which creates clarity for real work. Todoist is the trusted system; the weekly review is what keeps it trusted. Skip the review and the system degrades.

3. Next actions must be physical and visible. 'Work on the report' is not a next action. 'Open the file and write the introduction' is. GTD's demand for specificity isn't perfectionism — it's the difference between a task list that gets done and one that stays on the list forever.

## My reaction

> The 'open loops drain energy' framework explains why I feel mentally clearer after a good Todoist review than after a productive work session. The work does something; the review clears something. Both matter. The weekly review isn't just organization — it's the thing that makes the system trusted, which is what makes it work.
