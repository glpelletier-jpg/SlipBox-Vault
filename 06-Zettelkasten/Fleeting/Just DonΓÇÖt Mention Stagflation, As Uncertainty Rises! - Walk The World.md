> [!info]- Description On Wednesday we got the latest FOMC rate decision from Jerome Powell and the FED, following their first meeting since President Donald Trump’s sweeping tariff announcements last month.

He made it clear in the subsequent press conference that the US economy, based on the bulk of available data is still tracking well, and whilst the risks of seeing higher inflation and unemployment had risen, they held interest rates steady and significantly made clear they won’t be rushed into lowering borrowing costs until there’s more certainty on the direction of trade policy, which will have to come from the White House. So the benchmark federal funds rate stays in a range of 4.25% to 4.5%, where it’s been since December.

He did acknowledge that consumer and business sentiment had darkened amid the erratic tariff announcements, but this did not drive a rate change, preferring to see the outcomes and data ahead before acting. We can afford to be patient he said. He also observed that recent weaker GDP data might not be telling the whole story.

He underscored that uncertainty over the scope and scale of the tariffs — and the outcome of looming trade talks — will keep policymakers on hold for now and that they may have to make tough choices between lowering borrowing costs to support the job market or keeping them elevated to contain price pressures. The scenario of both higher inflation and higher unemployment is a nightmare for central bankers, because they on one hand would want lift rates to quash inflation but also cut rates to stimulate employment; the very recipe of stagflation, and the core problem with their dual mandate of both keeping inflation within band, and to ensure maximum employment.

He said “It’s not a situation where we can be pre-emptive because we actually don’t know what the right response to the data will be until we see more data,” We will be patient.

Other central bankers are more likely to cuts rates as the tariffs will act as a brake on already weak growth. The Bank of England, who meets today is likely to cut rates again in the UK, on the back of a slowing economy, despite inflation likely to be around 3%. And the RBA will meet later, perhaps with a similar thought in mind.

Money markets are fully priced for a move on May 20 to 3.85 per cent and are expecting more rate cuts than in the United States. Traders ascribe a total of 110 basis points of reductions by Christmas, which would take the benchmark under 3 per cent. In currencies, the Australian dollar lost some of its shine after scaling a five-month peak on Wednesday. It dropped a full cent, having jumped nearly 9 per cent in just a month.

But the key observation from the FED is how they are backed into a reactive corner, waiting to new data to update their models, and understanding that if stagflation does end up as the outcome, their playbook becomes very complicated indeed.
