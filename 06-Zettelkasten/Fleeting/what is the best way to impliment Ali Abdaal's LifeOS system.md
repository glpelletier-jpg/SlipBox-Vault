# what is the best way to impliment Ali Abdaal's LifeOS system

Here's a solid breakdown of how to implement Ali Abdaal's LifeOS system:

## The Two-Pillar Foundation

LifeOS is built on two core pillars: **Vision** (clarity on direction) and **Action** (frameworks to make progress). Most productivity training focuses exclusively on execution — LifeOS starts with Vision first, recognizing that efficient execution toward the wrong goals leads to burnout rather than fulfillment.

---

## The Six Core Components

LifeOS presents six integrated components that work together as an operating system: **Focused Hours** for daily execution, **Life Compass** for direction, **Productive Days** for daily structure, **Future Vision** for long-term planning, **Balanced Weeks** for rhythm, and **Quarterly Quests** for mid-term goals. Each component supports and reinforces the others.

---

## How to Implement It (Step by Step)

**1. Start with Vision (before anything else)**

The Vision pillar asks you to discover your core values and how they guide decisions, craft a compelling vision for your future, set meaningful goals aligned with your bigger purpose, and prioritize so you focus on high-leverage activities.

**2. Build Your Life Compass**

This is your reference point — a document or dashboard capturing your values, long-term vision, and key life areas. Think of it as your "why" before your "what."

**3. Set Quarterly Quests**

Rather than vague annual goals, break your vision into 90-day focused objectives. These are specific enough to act on but spacious enough to make real progress.

**4. Design Your Balanced Week**

One key concept is the "balanced week" — structuring your weeks to reflect your priorities rather than just reacting to demands. It's not about having a perfect week, but a realistic, intentional template.

**5. Build Productive Days with Focused Hours**

Time-block your most important work. Ali's system emphasizes tracking "focused hours" — deep work sessions — as your primary productivity metric rather than tasks completed.

**6. Run Weekly and Monthly Reviews**

Reflect on progress, analyze productivity metrics, and adjust your system weekly and monthly. This review cadence is what keeps the system alive and adaptive.

---

## Tools to Use

LifeOS focuses on principles and systems that can be implemented with any productivity apps you already use — there's no one required tool. That said, the most common setups are:

- **Notion** — for your Life Compass, Quarterly Quests, and goal tracking (Ali's official template is Notion-based)

- **Todoist or Things 3** — for daily task capture

- **Google Calendar** — for time blocking your week

- **A journal or notes app** — for weekly reviews

---

## The Key Mindset Shift

At its core, LifeOS is built on a philosophy that productivity should be enjoyable, sustainable, and meaningful — not about squeezing more tasks into your day, but a fundamental shift in how you approach time and energy.

The biggest implementation mistake people make is starting with the tools and tasks before clarifying their vision. Ali's system explicitly reverses that — values → vision → quarterly goals → weekly structure → daily execution. Get that order right and the rest flows naturally.

## The core rule

Use **collections** to group content within the *same* object type. Use **tags** to group content across *different* object types. That single distinction tells you everything — once you know it, the decision for each situation becomes obvious.

---

## Tags in LifeOS

Tags cut horizontally across your whole space. A tag surfaces *everything* related to a theme regardless of what type it is — a quest, a daily note, a book, and a weekly review can all share the same tag and appear together when you click it.

For LifeOS, keep your tag list tight. Suggested tags:

**Quarter tags** — apply to quests, weekly reviews, focus sessions, and books read that quarter:
`Q3-2026`, `Q4-2026` etc. Clicking the tag gives you an instant cross-type snapshot of everything that happened in that 90-day window.

**Life area tags** — for objects that don't have an Area property, or when you want to surface things across types:
`work`, `health`, `growth`, `relationships`

**Status tags** — light-touch flags:
`#someday` for ideas you're not acting on yet, `#reference` for notes that are purely for lookup

That's probably enough. The Capacities docs are explicit that tag bloat is a real problem — more tags means more maintenance and less meaning per tag. If you find yourself adding a new tag every week, that's a sign to stop and prune.

---

## Collections in LifeOS

Collections live *inside* a single object type and let you curate hand-picked subgroups. Content can belong to multiple collections within the same object type and can be moved easily — there is no need to silo information in one place.

Here's how to use them per type:

**Quest type collections:**

- `Active` — your current quarter's quests (fast access without filtering)

- `Completed` — archive of finished quests, useful for quarterly reviews

- `Someday` — ideas for future quarters you don't want to lose

**Weekly Review type collections:**

- `Q3-2026`, `Q4-2026` — one per quarter, so you can browse a full quarter's reviews side by side

**Book type collections:**

- `Currently reading`

- `Want to read`

- `Finished` (or use a sub-collection per year: `Read in 2026`)

**Focus Session type collections:**

- Not usually needed here — the linked Quest property handles grouping better than a collection would

---

## The practical decision tree

When you're about to organise something, ask:

1. **Is it all the same object type?** → use a collection

2. **Does it span multiple types?** → use a tag

3. **Is it structural/permanent** (like "Completed" quests)? → collection

4. **Is it temporal/cross-cutting** (like "Q3-2026")? → tag on everything, plus a collection within the Quest type for fast access

One nuance worth knowing: collections let you zoom in on a subset of one object type while keeping all the filtering, sorting, and view options that object type supports. So if you want to switch your Q3 quests into a kanban board view or a gallery, a collection lets you do that — a tag alone won't.

---

## What to avoid

Don't recreate your object type structure with tags. If you already have a Quest type with an `Area` property set to "Health", you don't also need a `#health` tag on every quest — the property already does that job and you can filter by it. Tags are for spanning *across* types, not duplicating properties you already have.
