# Zettelkasten Notebook

The Morning Brain Dump 

The first ten minutes of a working day are often the richest time for capturing floating thoughts. During sleep and in the hours before full engagement with tasks, the mind makes connections that it does not make under directed attention. The morning brain dump captures whatever has accumulated. 

The method is simple: open your notebook to a fresh page, write the date, and spend ten minutes writing whatever is present. No filter, no organization, no judgment. Questions you are sitting with. Ideas that arrived half-formed. Things you read yesterday that are still active. Arguments you want to make. Problems that are not resolved. 



EXAMPLE: A MORNING BRAIN DUMP PAGE 

Date: [any working morning] 

"Still thinking about the chapter on attention I read yesterday — the bit about directed vs undirected attention felt important but I couldn't articulate why. Something about how the default mode network needs non-directed time? Check this. 



The meeting yesterday raised an interesting question about how we communicate uncertainty to clients — we seem to have two different approaches depending on the client and I'm not sure which one we default to or why. 



Is there a connection between how Luhmann describes his slip box as a 'conversation partner' and what I've been reading about extended cognition? Both seem to be saying that thinking can happen outside the skull." 



*Note: This page is messy, associative, and incomplete. It is supposed to be.*



Capturing While Reading

 Reading capture has one rule that overrides everything else: do not interrupt your reading to write good notes. The goal is to stay in the flow of reading while leaving yourself enough material to work with later. 

The method: read a chapter or section without stopping. When you encounter something that activates a thought — not just something worth remembering, but something worth thinking about — underline it or dog-ear the page. At the end of the chapter, spend three to five minutes writing the ideas that are still active. Not everything you underlined. The things that are still present in your mind, in your own words.



 EXAMPLE: READING CAPTURE SESSION 

Scenario: someone reads a chapter of a book about communication on a train. At the chapter's end, they write: 

"The distinction between transmission and ritual models of communication seems important — transmission sees communication as information transfer, ritual sees it as the creation of shared reality. Most business communication is designed on the transmission model but actually depends on the ritual model to work? 



If this is right then the reason our meeting communication often fails isn't because the information wasn't delivered clearly — it's because the ritual conditions weren't created. Worth thinking about." 



*This took four minutes to write. It captures a genuine thought, not a summary of the chapter. That is the difference.*



The Full Scenario: A Study Session From Start to Finish 

To make the capture ritual concrete, here is a complete study session walked through in real time. The scenario: a university student with two hours, a textbook chapter to cover, and an essay due in three weeks. 



Open the notebook to a fresh page. Write the date and the source: "Chapter 4 — [textbook title]." This takes twenty seconds.  

Read the chapter without stopping to write. Underline or fold pages where something activates a thought. The goal is comprehension first, capture second.  

At the end of the chapter, close the book. Write, without looking back, the three to five ideas that are still present. These are the ideas worth developing — if they have stayed in your mind through the whole chapter, they are meaningful to you. 

Now skim the underlined passages. For any underlined passage that corresponds to an idea you did not write in step 3, ask: is this actually interesting to me, or was I just being conscientious? If it is genuinely interesting, add a brief note. If not, leave it.  

At the bottom of the page, write one question the chapter raised. Not a question for the exam — a question you are actually curious about. This question is often where the best permanent notes come from. 



 *The entire post-reading capture session takes between five and ten minutes. The output is a page of raw material — imperfect, associative, yours — that is ready to be processed in this week's processing session.*
