# I never lost experiments — I lost the reasons behind them.



![Tracing Experimental Decisions and Their Underlying Rationales](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:640/format:webp/1*4dusPiYRPHw3jYXHf7lYLw.png)
Tracing Experimental Decisions and Their Underlying Rationales - Notes

For a long time, my research history looked deceptively clean.

I had experiment folders.I had scripts with version numbers.I had plots saved with careful filenames.

And yet, every time I returned to an old experiment, the same question came up:

*Why did I even do this?*

Not *how* — that was usually clear from the code.But **why this experiment, at this time, in this way**.

That question is surprisingly hard to answer once the context is gone.

## Experiments don’t explain themselves

We like to believe experiments are self-justifying.

They produce results.Results speak.End of story.

Except they don’t.

An experiment without its decision context is just an artifact. It doesn’t tell you:

- which alternatives were considered

- which assumptions were accepted

- which constraints forced your hand

- which question you were actually trying to answer

So when an experiment fails, or when a reviewer questions it, you’re left reconstructing history from fragments.

That’s exhausting — and often inaccurate.

## The missing layer: decision traceability

The core problem wasn’t documentation. It was **traceability**.

I could trace:

- code → results

But I couldn’t trace:

- question → decision → experiment

Those links lived only in my head. And my head is a terrible long-term storage device.

So I started treating decisions as first-class research objects.

## From idea to experiment is not a jump — it’s a chain

In reality, experiments don’t come from ideas directly.

They come from a chain that looks more like this:

- question

- assumption

- constraint

- decision

- experiment

If you only document the last step, you lose the logic that made it reasonable.

And once that logic is gone, experiments start to look arbitrary — even to you.

## The simple change: linking decisions to experiments

The fix wasn’t complicated.

For every experiment that mattered, I made sure I could answer one question in Obsidian:

> *“Which decision led to this experiment?”*

Sometimes that meant creating a short decision note *after* the experiment already existed.

Other times, it meant writing the decision note first and linking the experiment to it.

The format didn’t matter. The **connection** did.

## What a decision-to-experiment link looks like

A typical setup now looks like this:

- A **decision note** explains:

- what choice was made

- why

- under which assumptions

- An **experiment note or folder** links back to that decision

- Failures or unexpected results link back again

This creates a loop:decision → experiment → outcome → revised decision

That loop is the real unit of research progress.

## Why this matters more than clean experiment logs

Clean logs help with reproducibility.Decision traces help with **understanding**.

When a reviewer asks:

- “Why didn’t you try method B?”

- “Why is this parameter fixed?”

- “Why is this dataset appropriate?”

Those are decision questions, not technical ones.

Without decision traces, you answer from memory. With them, you answer from evidence — your own reasoning, preserved in time.

## Rediscovering old experiments without self-loathing

There is a special kind of frustration in reopening old work and thinking:*What was I thinking?*

Decision traces change that.

Instead of judging past-you, you see:

- what information you had

- what constraints existed

- why the decision made sense then

Sometimes you still conclude it was wrong. But at least you know *why* it happened.

That’s much kinder — and much more useful.

## Obsidian turns history into navigation

What makes Obsidian work here is not the notes themselves, but the ability to **move through reasoning**.

I can start at:

- a failed experiment

And navigate to:

- the decision behind it

- the assumption that broke

- the earlier idea that motivated it

This turns my vault into a research timeline — not chronological, but logical.

That’s far more valuable than a date-based history.

## This prevents accidental repetition

One of the quiet benefits: I stopped repeating myself.

Before, I would revisit an idea, convinced it was new — only to rediscover later that I had already tried something similar.

Now, when a new idea feels familiar, I search for the decision, not the experiment.

If a similar decision exists, I immediately see:

- why it led to a certain experiment

- why it failed or succeeded

That saves weeks.

## Decision traces also expose weak thinking

This system is not always comfortable.

When you try to write down why you chose something, gaps appear:

- vague justifications

- implicit preferences

- decisions made out of convenience

That’s not a failure of the system — that’s feedback.

Weak decisions are easier to improve *before* they turn into weak experiments.

## Writing becomes easier (again)

Late in the PhD, writing methods sections used to feel like reverse engineering.

Now it’s closer to translation.

I don’t invent a clean narrative. I compress an existing one:

- from decision notes

- from linked assumptions

- from experiment outcomes

The story already exists. I just adapt it to academic language.

That reduces both effort and anxiety.

## This also helps when things go wrong

When an experiment fails spectacularly, it’s tempting to bury it.

Decision traces make failures useful.

You can see:

- whether the failure invalidates the decision

- or only a specific implementation

- or an assumption that can be adjusted

That keeps failure local instead of existential.

## What this is not

This is not about bureaucracy.

You’re not creating paperwork for future audits.You’re creating **cognitive continuity**.

Most decision notes are short. Some are messy. Some are wrong.

That’s fine.

They exist to preserve *why*, not to be correct forever.

## A rule that helped me

Here’s the rule I use now:

> *If an experiment took more than a day of work, it deserves a linked decision.*

That’s it.

Not every idea needs a trace. But every costly action does.

## The deeper benefit: agency

Tracing decisions changed how I feel about my work.

Research stopped feeling like a series of reactions — to data, to feedback, to reviewers.

It started feeling like a sequence of **choices**.

Even constrained choices are still choices.

That perspective matters a lot in long projects where motivation fluctuates.

## Final reflection

Experiments are visible.Decisions are not.

But decisions are where research actually happens.

Obsidian didn’t help me run better experiments.It helped me **understand why I run the experiments I do**.

If you ever look at your own results and feel oddly disconnected from them, try tracing one experiment back to its decision.

You might rediscover not just your reasoning — but your original intent.

And that’s often what gets lost first.
