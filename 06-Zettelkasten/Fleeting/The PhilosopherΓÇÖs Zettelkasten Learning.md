### Learning

Everyone is in favor of learning. This near-universal approbation ought to make us suspicious.

When a thing is universally praised, it is usually because it has been defined so loosely that no one feels its edges. Liberty is praised by the tyrant and the prisoner alike, because each means something entirely different by the word and neither has examined the word closely enough to notice. Virtue is praised by the saint and the hypocrite with equal warmth, for similar reasons. And learning – learning is praised by the scholar who has devoted his life to it, the merchant who has avoided it entirely, and the politician who has weaponized its appearance, all with complete sincerity, because each has a different idea of what it is and is confident the others share it.

Let us, then, attempt to find its edges.

### Of What Learning Is Not, Which Is the More Tractable Question

Learning is not the accumulation of information. This distinction is presently urgent, in an age when information is available in quantities that would have stupefied Aristotle and does, in fact, stupefy a considerable portion of the contemporary population without producing any noticeable wisdom in return.

The man who has read ten thousand articles and can recall the conclusion of each has done something. He has stocked a warehouse. Whether the warehouse constitutes a mind depends on what he has done with the contents – whether he has arranged them, questioned them, set them in conflict with one another, and asked what they mean taken together rather than separately. Information that has not been questioned is not knowledge. It is inventory.

Nor is learning the possession of credentials, though the two are frequently confused and the confusion is profitable to those who manufacture credentials. The diploma announces that its holder has satisfied certain requirements over a certain period of time. What it does not announce – what it cannot announce, having no way to know – is whether anything happened inside the holder’s mind during this period, or whether the requirements were satisfied by a kind of elaborate performance that left the mind unchanged. Both are possible. Both occur regularly. The ceremony is identical.

Nor is learning, though this will disappoint those who have suffered greatly in its pursuit, the same as intelligence. One may be highly intelligent and learn very little, if intelligence is deployed primarily in the service of protecting existing beliefs from revision. This is, in fact, one of the more efficient uses of high intelligence – the construction of elaborate justifications for what one already thinks, which is intellectually demanding work and produces nothing new. The intelligent man who has not learned to be uncomfortable has wasted a considerable natural resource.

### Of Schools, Which Are Institutions for Learning and Sometimes Achieve It

The school is a peculiar invention. It proceeds from the reasonable observation that children require guidance in learning what prior generations have discovered, since it would be inefficient for each generation to rediscover fire, mathematics, and the inadvisability of touching certain plants. On this foundation it builds an edifice of considerable complexity, which often achieves the reasonable observation’s aim and occasionally defeats it entirely.

The school gathers children of similar age – who are, by virtue of similar age, at similar stages of development but wildly dissimilar in temperament, curiosity, and the particular directions in which their minds naturally move – and presents to all of them the same material in the same sequence at the same pace. The child who is too fast grows bored and either self-educates or concludes that learning is boring. The child who is too slow grows anxious and either perseveres with great effort or concludes that learning is for others. The child who is correctly paced thrives, and we credit the institution.

The subjects taught in schools reflect, more or less, what the people who designed the curriculum thought was important at the time the curriculum was designed, which is to say they reflect the past’s best guess about what the future would need. Since the future has the inconsiderate habit of not resembling the past, this involves inherent error, which accumulates over decades as the curriculum is revised slowly and the world changes quickly, producing at any given moment a gap of variable but never negligible size between what is taught and what is needed.

This is not an argument against schools. It is an argument for understanding what schools can and cannot do, which schools themselves rarely teach, since the proposition that school is limited in what it teaches is not a proposition schools are institutionally inclined to emphasize.

What schools can do, at their best, is not teach any particular content but teach the habit of learning – the tolerance for difficulty, the patience with confusion, the recognition that not-knowing is the beginning of the process rather than an embarrassment to be concealed. The teacher who accomplishes this has accomplished more than the teacher who has transferred the most information, whose students will forget the information and retain, if they are lucky, the habit.

### Of Books, Which Are the Greatest Educators and Also Magnificent Instruments of Confirmed Prejudice

The book is humanity’s most successful technology for the transmission of thought. I write this aware that it requires defense, in an age of competing technologies that transmit thought with greater speed and lesser depth in roughly equal measure.

What distinguishes the book is duration. The book requires that thought be sustained – that an argument be followed across many pages, that a conclusion wait until the evidence has been presented, that the reader remain in the company of an idea long enough to understand it rather than merely encounter it. This is not comfortable. It is not, by the standards of more stimulating entertainments, exciting. It is, however, approximately what thinking requires.

And yet the book, too, can deceive. The reader who reads only books that confirm what he already believes has used the greatest educational technology to insulate himself from education. He has surrounded himself with articulate agreement and called it reading. This is not learning. This is interior decoration.

The book educates only when it presents the reader with something the reader did not know, or did not believe, or did not know he did not know. The book that tells you only what you think, more eloquently than you would have thought it, produces pleasure and produces nothing else. There is a place for such books. That place is not in a philosophy of learning.

The genuine encounter with a book – the encounter that constitutes learning – involves, at some point, resistance. The reader who agrees with everything in a book has either found a perfect book or has not read carefully. The latter is more probable. The reader who disagrees violently with a book, and has tested his disagreement, and understood precisely why he disagrees, has been educated regardless of whether the book was right, because he now knows something about both the book’s argument and the architecture of his own.

This is why the burning of books is not merely tyranny but stupidity – not merely the suppression of particular ideas but the elimination of the friction that makes thought move.

### Of Experience, Which Everyone Credits and No One Quite Trusts

We say that experience is the great teacher. We say this with such regularity that we have ceased to examine whether it is true.

Experience teaches, certainly. But what it teaches depends almost entirely on what the learner brings to it. The same experience, undergone by two persons of different preparation and disposition, produces two entirely different lessons – and sometimes two lessons that are precisely opposite. The man who fails in his first business may learn that he chose the wrong business, or that he executed poorly, or that business is not for him, or that the world is rigged against honest men, or that he requires a partner with different strengths, or that failure is not permanent. He will learn whichever of these his prior convictions, his emotional state, and his current needs make available to him. Experience will deliver the verdict his mind was inclined to reach.

This is not nothing. The experience confirms, refines, and makes vivid what might otherwise have remained abstract. But it does not, by itself, correct. The man who enters experience with a wrong model of the world will very often emerge from experience with the same wrong model, annotated.

The learner who extracts genuine learning from experience is the learner who enters it with questions – who has decided in advance what he is trying to discover, who observes rather than merely undergoes, who is willing to have his prediction disconfirmed, and who regards disconfirmation as information rather than as injury. This, too, is a learned skill. Experience teaches us to learn from experience only to the degree that we have already learned how to learn.

This circularity is real and is not a paradox but a description of how growth works – spiraling, each pass adding something, no single revolution completing the process.

### Of the Teacher, Who Is Sometimes Necessary and Sometimes an Obstacle

The great teacher is among the rarest of beings, which is unfortunate, since the position is among the most consequential. A great general changes the outcomes of a few battles. A great statesman shapes a country for a generation. A great teacher shapes the minds that will shape everything else, which is to say a great teacher is the upstream cause of most of what matters, and is paid accordingly – which is to say, poorly.

What does the great teacher do? Not, primarily, transfer information. Information can now be transferred by machines, and eventually machines will do it better than all but the finest teachers. What the great teacher does is harder to mechanize: she creates the conditions under which the student’s mind encounters an idea as a genuine problem rather than an answer to be memorized. She makes the student feel the weight of the question before offering any relief of resolution. She models, visibly and explicitly, the process of not-knowing-yet – the productive confusion, the partial hypothesis, the willingness to be wrong in public – so that the student understands that this process is not a failure of intelligence but its exercise.

The great teacher teaches not content but relationship – the student’s relationship to difficulty, to uncertainty, to other minds, to the past that has accumulated in books and the future that will require something beyond what books contain.

The mediocre teacher, who is the common variety, transmits what she knows in the hope that it will be received as she received it, and is perpetually disappointed that it is not, because transmission without transformation is not teaching but broadcasting, and the student is under no obligation to be a good receiver.

The bad teacher does something worse. The bad teacher teaches the student that learning is what happens in this room, under these conditions, toward these examinations, after which it concludes. This lesson, once learned, is very difficult to unlearn. There are adults who have not read a serious book since leaving school, not because they are incapable, but because school successfully taught them that reading serious books was a school activity, now behind them, like uniform and homework and being told when they could use the lavatory. The bad teacher has produced a person whose learning stopped at the school gate and has not resumed.

This is a significant crime, committed without malice, by persons who were themselves often victims of the same crime.

### Of Unlearning, Which Is the Hardest Learning of All

We speak of learning as acquisition – as the adding of something to the mind that was not there before. This is correct but incomplete.

The more demanding form of learning is not addition but revision – the discovery that something one believed was wrong, and the consequent necessity of dismantling the belief and reconstructing something more accurate in its place. This is unlearning, and it is harder than learning by an amount that is not small.

Learning something new meets no internal resistance, provided the new thing does not contradict something already held. The mind can receive new information about the habits of penguins, the history of the Byzantine Empire, or the chemical properties of bismuth without any disruption to its existing contents, since it had no prior strong beliefs about these matters, and files the new information in uncontested territory.

But unlearning – the revision of what was already believed – encounters the full resistance of the existing structure. The prior belief is not merely an idea in isolation. It is connected to other beliefs, to memories, to identity, to the face one shows the world. To abandon it is to pull a thread that unravels more than the thread. This is why intelligent people cling to wrong beliefs long past the point where the evidence has deserted them – not stupidity but self-preservation. The architecture of conviction protects itself.

The person who has genuinely unlearned something important – who has revised a central belief in response to evidence, at the cost of the discomfort that revision entails – has performed an act of intellectual courage that no mere accumulation of new facts requires. She has earned the right to say she has learned, in the fullest sense. She is, in some meaningful way, a different person than she was, because she has changed not merely the contents of her mind but its structure.

This should be celebrated. It is usually not. We celebrate consistency – the man who believed X in youth and believes X in age is called principled. The man who believed X in youth and believes Y in age, because he examined X and found it insufficient, is called unreliable. Our social epistemology rewards stubbornness and calls it integrity.

### Of the Relationship Between Learning and Humility, Which Are Almost the Same Thing

The more one learns, the more one discovers how much remains to be learned – not merely in the sense of unexplored territory, but in the sense that the explored territory turns out to be less well-explored than it appeared. Every subject, approached with sufficient depth, reveals beneath its apparent certainties a stratum of genuine uncertainty, contested interpretation, and questions that the experts have not resolved and in some cases have barely noticed.

This is why genuine learning reliably produces humility, and the absence of humility is a reliable sign of incomplete learning. The man who has read one book on economics is certain about economic policy. The man who has read twenty books is aware that the economists disagree. The man who has read two hundred has opinions of his own that he holds tentatively, aware of the counterarguments, aware that the field itself is aware of its limitations. The man who has spent a lifetime in economics returns, at the end, to a kind of learned uncertainty that looks, from the outside, dangerously similar to where the first man started – but is as different from it as a color seen by an artist and the same color seen by a man who does not know it has a name.

This is why the loudest, most confident voices on any complex subject are frequently the least informed about it. Confidence of this kind is not a sign of mastery but of insufficient contact with the material – insufficient contact to have discovered how hard the material is, how much has been thought before, how many smart people have reached different conclusions from similar evidence.

The beginner mistakes the surface for the depth. The expert knows how deep it goes.

### Conclusion, Held With Appropriate Tentativeness

What, then, is learning?

It is the process by which the mind becomes less wrong about the world, and becomes aware of being less wrong, and becomes aware that being less wrong is a direction rather than a destination. It involves the accumulation of knowledge and the revision of knowledge and the relinquishing of knowledge that turns out to have been comfortable error. It requires friction – with books that resist, teachers who refuse to make it easy, experiences that contradict, and other minds that will not agree. It produces, in its mature form, not certainty but a more sophisticated uncertainty – the uncertainty of someone who knows the shape of what they do not know, which is entirely different from the uncertainty of someone who has never thought about it.

It has no terminus. The person who believes she has finished learning has finished learning, which is among the saddest voluntary decisions available to a conscious being.

And it is not – this bears repeating, since the confusion is constant and consequential – it is not the same as schooling, or credentials, or intelligence, or the ability to retrieve information quickly, or the confidence with which one holds one’s present opinions. It is something rarer and less comfortable than all of these: the genuine, repeated willingness to be changed by what one encounters.

This willingness is not natural. The natural condition of the mind is to protect what it already contains. Learning, properly understood, is the practice of overriding this protection regularly, deliberately, and with the understanding that what floods in will rearrange the furniture permanently.

Most people prefer their furniture where it is.

The ones who do not – they are what learning produces, when it succeeds.
