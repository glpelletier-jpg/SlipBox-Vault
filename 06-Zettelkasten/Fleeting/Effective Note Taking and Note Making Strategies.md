# Note Taking

## Types of Notes

Generally speaking you'll encounter three main types of notes in your Second Brain System.

1. Fleeting Notes

2. [[Literature Notes]]

3. Distilled Notes also known as Permanent Notes

> [!info] What is a Fleeting Note? A "fleeting note" 🌱 is a brief, temporary note or piece of information that is not meant to be saved or retained long-term.

> [!info] What is a Literature Note? A "literature note"🌿refers to a specific type of note that is used to capture and organise information from external sources, such as books, articles, research papers, YouTube videos, etc.

> [!info] What is a Distilled Note? A "distilled note" ⚗️ refers to a specific type of note that is designed to be a long-lasting, evergreen🌲 piece of content within your knowledge base.

While Fleeting Notes, Literature Notes, and Distilled (Permanent) Notes are common in Personal Knowledge Management (PKM) systems like Obsidian or #zettelkasten, there are other types of notes that can be utilised. Some additional types include:

1. **Project Notes**: Notes specifically related to ongoing projects, including plans, timelines, and progress updates.

2. **Meeting Notes**: Summaries and key points from meetings, often including action items and decisions made.

3. **Reference Notes**: Notes that serve as references, containing information such as procedures, standards, and important data.

4. **Idea Notes**: Notes capturing random ideas, brainstorming sessions, or potential future projects and articles.

5. **Concept Notes**: Notes that capture and develop abstract ideas or theories, often linking related concepts together for deeper understanding.

6. **Daily Notes**: Journaling or daily log entries that capture thoughts, reflections, and daily activities.

7. **Task Notes**: To-do lists and task management notes, often integrated with productivity systems.

8. **Research Notes**: Detailed notes from research, including data, findings, and analysis.

9. **Quote Notes**: Collections of quotes from various sources, often tagged for easy retrieval.

10. **Learning Notes**: Notes from courses, workshops, or self-study, summarising key learnings and takeaways.

These various types of notes help create a comprehensive and organised knowledge management system, catering to different aspects of personal and professional life.

# Note Making

## Think of your notes as a personal knowledge garden

> [!info] A Metaphor for Note Making Think of new notes that enter your system as seedings. As they grow they develop into plants. Evergreen notes are your final distilled note.

[image: Note-Making-Cycle.png]

## Note Making Stages

1. **Seedling Stage:** 🌱

    - Consider adding more detail or context when capturing new notes. The more information you can include initially, the easier it will be to cultivate those notes later.

    - Experiment with different note-taking formats (e.g., bullet points, mind maps, outlines) to see what works best for you in the seedling stage.

    - Establish a system for tagging or categorising your seedling notes to make them easier to locate and connect later on.

2. **Growth and Development:** 🌿

    - Regularly review your seedling notes and identify opportunities to expand, refine, or combine them into more robust "plants."

    - Look for connections between your notes and try to cross-pollinate ideas by linking related concepts.

    - Consider adding visual elements (e.g., diagrams, images) to help solidify your understanding and make the notes more engaging.

3. **Evergreen Notes:** 🌲

    - Continuously refine and distil your evergreen notes to ensure they are concise, comprehensive, and up-to-date.

    - Regularly review your evergreen notes to identify areas that could benefit from further refinement or expansion.

    - Explore ways to make your evergreen notes more actionable, such as including next steps or practical applications.

4. **Knowledge Garden Maintenance:** ℹ️

    - Periodically prune or remove outdated or redundant notes to keep your knowledge garden tidy and manageable.

    - Experiment with different organizational structures (e.g., hierarchical, network-based) to see what works best for your learning style and the type of information you're working with.

    - Consider ways to share or collaborate on your knowledge garden with others, as this can help cross-pollinate ideas and strengthen your understanding.

> [!tip] Remember, the note-making process is highly personal, so feel free to adapt and customise this metaphor to fit your unique needs and preferences. The key is to find a system that helps you effectively capture, organize, and distil your knowledge over time.
