## Why Obsidian is the Perfect Foundation

After testing dozens of knowledge management tools — from Roam Research to Notion, Tana, Reflect, Mem.ai, Constella, Capacities, SuperNotes, Kinopio, Heptabase, Scrintal, LogSeq to Remnote — Obsidian emerged as the clear winner for four critical reasons:

## 1. Local-First Philosophy: Complete Data Ownership

Your notes live on your device in simple Markdown files. No vendor lock-in, no subscription anxiety, no “what happens if the company shuts down” concerns. I can open any note with any text editor, now or 20 years from now. This isn’t just philosophical — it’s practical. When you’re building enterprise software across 8 countries, data sovereignty matters.

## 2. Markdown Format: AI-Friendly and Future-Proof

Every major AI system understands Markdown perfectly. When Claude Code reads my notes, it sees clean, structured content without proprietary formatting noise. This creates a seamless bridge between human-readable content and AI processing.

## 3. Cross-Platform Excellence That Actually Works

The iOS, Android, and desktop apps are blazingly fast and feature-complete. I can capture thoughts on my iPhone while traveling between C&C offices in Stockholm, edit on my iPad while waiting for flights, and do deep work on my MacBook in Puglia. The sync is instant and reliable.

**The Mobile Revolution Nobody Talks About**

Here’s what most PKM tools get wrong: mobile isn’t an afterthought — it’s where 60% of ideas are born. I’ve tested everything from Notion to Tana, and while Tana is powerful on desktop, its mobile experience is sluggish and frustrating. Obsidian? It’s a different story entirely.

The mobile app is a genuine masterpiece of engineering. Real-time sync that actually works in milliseconds. Full feature parity with desktop. Native performance that feels like a first-party Apple app. When I’m in a meeting with partners in Milan and need to quickly capture an insight, I don’t want to wrestle with a laggy interface. I need something that gets out of my way and lets me think.

This isn’t just convenience — it’s critical for knowledge capture. Ideas don’t wait for you to be at your desk. They come during commutes, in coffee shops, between meetings. A second brain that only works at your desk is only half a brain.

**The Simplicity Principle**

Here’s the trap most people fall into: they create elaborate property systems with 50 fields and complex hierarchies. Then they open the app on their phone, see all those required fields, and… close it again. The friction kills the habit.

My rule: if I can’t add a note in under 10 seconds on mobile, the system is too complex. Tags are instant, intuitive, and flexible. No mandatory fields, no complex forms, just capture the thought and add context with a simple.

This simplicity is what makes the system sustainable. After 18 months, I still use it daily because it doesn’t fight me. It adapts to how I think, not the other way around. Remember: the best system is the one you actually use, not the one with the most features.

This creates natural hierarchies without rigid structures. My “Alek Dobrohotov” note (#human) connects to “alekdob.com” (#project), which links to specific goals (#goal) and actionable todos (#todo). The system breathes and evolves organically.

## Double Bracket Linking: Where Connections Come Alive

Obsidian’s _double bracke_t linking is where the magic happens. Instead of filing notes in folders, I create relationships between concepts. When I write about implementing OAuth in our Flow ERP, that note naturally links to:

- *Supabase Authentication* (technical implementation)

- *C&C Security Requirements* (business context)

- *User Experience Design* (product considerations)

- *International Compliance* (regulatory aspects)

These connections create a web of knowledge that mirrors how we actually think — through associations and relationships, not rigid categories.

## The Claude Code Integration: Where Magic Happens

This is where my second brain becomes truly intelligent. Claude Code doesn’t just see my code — it understands my entire context. Here’s how I set it up:

## Technical Setup (5 Minutes)

1. Open your Obsidian vault folder with your preferred IDE or terminal

2. Run `claude` from terminal to initialize Claude Code in your project

3. Run `init` to create the CLAUDE.md file where you can:

- Add crucial information about yourself

- Link to important files that define who you are

- Explain your tag system and what each tag means

- Describe your folder structure

- Note that you use Zettelkasten methodology

- Provide any context that helps Claude Code navigate and search effectively

The CLAUDE.md file becomes your AI’s instruction manual. You can write whatever you want — this is where you teach Claude Code how to understand your specific system.

## Essential Starting Principles

Start small: Begin with simple notes about your current projects. Don’t try to backfill years of information immediately.

Link liberally: When in doubt, create a connection. Over-linking is rarely a problem; under-linking always is.

Think in concepts: Each note should contain one main idea that could be useful in multiple contexts.

Trust the process: The real value emerges after you have 50+ connected notes. Be patient with the initial setup period.
