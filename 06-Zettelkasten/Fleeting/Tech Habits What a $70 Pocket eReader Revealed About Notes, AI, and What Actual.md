# Tech Habits: What a $70 Pocket eReader Revealed About Notes, AI, and What Actually Matters

I was drowning in content before AI, but AI has definitely magnified this effect. Writing something used to require putting pen to paper, or at least sitting with a blank screen long enough to wrestle with it. That friction is mostly gone now. I can produce more in an afternoon than I could in a week not long ago. To exacerbate the situation, I can fill almost any time of the day consuming content in more ways than I ever could.

And so can everyone else.

For me, this makes the question of what deserves my attention more important than ever.

It’s exhausting and even unnatural to retreat into purity, where only the finest, most considered thoughts make it in front of you. The world is messy. You have to ride the waves that come. But you also want the signal to actually be signal. You want what you keep, what you read, what you put out there, to mean something authentic to you.

I didn’t expect to find a clearer way of thinking about this in a $70 pocket-sized eReader. But I guess that is what happened.

I bought the [Xteink X4](https://www.xteink.com/products/xteink-x4/?utm_source=google&utm_medium=paid&utm_campaign=sa_us&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=23217504713&gbraid=0AAAABBaZUiUJf5uzmDJbyA32WIQceJwp4&gclid=Cj0KCQjw-pHPBhCdARIsAHXYWP9TQk3kIb9jw9kQZA-tRY1jflpKPihr5KBeKb_vxnZhcWJm2ZaJqA0aAtXaEALw_wcB) (X4) on a whim a few weeks ago. I had Amazon credit, the device looked playful, and I have severe enough myopia that long stretches with a Kindle or a phone screen tire my eyes out. Audiobooks have carried most of my reading life for years, but I missed the slowness of actual reading.

So I bought it expecting maybe a small upgrade to my reading habits. Maybe I’d use this during interstitial periods of time as an alternative to putting on my Airpods and listening to an audiobook. What I did not expect was that this device would sharpen the way I think about how I take notes, write, and the role of AI.

The X4 may have just made the cut to be on my everyday carry list: iPhone, Airpods, Wallet, Keys, Swiss Army Knifeish thing, Pocket Bullet Journal.

It is small, weighs almost nothing, and single purpose. It is just an e-ink screen and what I have chosen to put on it.

I (easily!) flashed the [CrossPoint Reader firmware](https://github.com/crosspoint-reader/crosspoint-reader) onto it, which transformed the experience. The interface became cleaner. The typography more thoughtful. And there is a small detail I did not think would matter: when the device is off, it displays the cover art of whatever I am currently reading, emphasizing the single-purposesness of the whole thing.

CrossPoint also lets the device act as a local access point for loading files. I can easily connect to it from my phone or laptop. There is just enough friction in this to be useful. I cannot mindlessly sync everything, so I have to decide.

And yes, for us plain text enthusiasts, `.txt` and `.md` files load just fine and are just about as plain as you would expect.

For me, this is where the experience diverges from a phone or a synced cloud notes app. Everything cannot and should not live here. The friction of loading something onto this device forces a question every time: does this belong here?

Like the OG iPod or devices of its ilk, that question is the whole thing. Most of the time, the answer is no. Most of the rough captures, the half-thoughts, the bullet point lists, the AI-generated summaries do not belong on a device whose entire purpose is undistracted reading. They belong in capture systems where speed matters more than care.

But occasionally something does belong. And recognizing what does and does not is what this post is about.

The things I want on the device are almost entirely human-shaped. Books I am actually reading. Texts I want to sit with. And, increasingly, the main documents from my own [notable folders](https://medium.com/@geetduggal/this-one-idea-changed-the-way-i-organized-my-notes-forever-f293371a0d5b): information on Wikipedia-like people, projects, and spaces I have already curated into sentences worth re-reading.

A small example. We are doing a light refresh of our living room: essentially new shelving surrounding the TV. The whole process is both a reflection of our actual budget and a recognition that the family lives in this space. My wife’s books, my books, the kids’ books and toys, the usual stuff in an entertainment center. The room is not mine alone to design and I learned how to better communicate with my wife about this kind of stuff in the midst of a hectic schedule with kids. Yet designing it still satisfies at a high level the feeling of a classic home library wall-to-wall-ish, ceiling-to-ceiling-ish. The kind you would see in universities or fantasy films. It brought back warm feelings of remodeling our kitchen in a previous home and similar decisions that needed to be made regarding dimensions, lighting, delivery logistics, etc.

I wrote that whole thing up. Not as bullet points. As prose. How I was thinking about the trade-offs, what I cared about, what mattered about the constraints rather than in spite of them. I put it on the eReader for giggles since it was on my mind. When I was actually at the store, I pulled it out and read through it as I was making decisions. Not as a checklist. As a way of remembering what I had already decided I cared about while making more fine-grained decisions.

That experience was the moment something clicked. Reading my own distilled prose on this device, in a bounded context with no other pull, was very different than reading it on a phone or in Obsidian. The sentences worked because they had been shaped by me, and the device let me absorb them without competition.

Critically, those sentences in my notable folder didn’t just appear from a blank page. They were a result of casually revisiting transcripts of my voice journals. First, placing in bold stand-out phrases and sentences, and then highlighting what stands out from the bold.

The method behind that prose is not new. Tiago Forte calls it [progressive summarization](https://fortelabs.com/blog/progressive-summarization-a-practical-technique-for-designing-discoverable-notes/). And my particular flavor of it for interstitial journaling is called ‘[distilled interstitial journaling](https://medium.com/@geetduggal/capture-to-do-d040e1bae4a6)’. You bold the parts of a source that resonate. Then you highlight the bolds. Then, if a piece earns it, you craft a few sentences in your own voice. Each layer is more compressed, more personal, and more useful for future you.

The critical part is that it is done by hand. You stay tethered to the source the whole time. You can drill back down. The marking process is itself the discovery.

This is the step that AI summaries skip. An AI can give you a clean summary of a conversation or an article, and that summary may be accurate in some sense, but it will not be *yours*. I’ve observed that the act of deciding what matters is when key insights and self-authenticity materialize. Outsource that step and you get tidy notes that may feel hollow when you go back to them.

Taking it a step further, you may even ask AI to progressively summarize! I’d argue that’s better than AI simply summarizing a voice transcript. You can at least *see* what it thinks is valuable on multiple scales against your transcript. Interestingly, it may be different than what *you* think is valuable. And that’s the point.

Taking a step back, I think there is a difference between things you want to vibe code / write and things you want to craft.

For example, I have recently used AI to generate a [Linear calendar extension for an Obsidian](https://medium.com/@geetduggal/tech-habits-property-folders-a-linear-calendar-view-in-obsidian-a-plain-text-renaissance-9252bad51a46) plugin I rely on daily. I do not really care about the internals (at the moment). I described the parameters, after some iteration it produced something that worked swimmingly, and that was the win. The product was the UX I transactionally requested. The code itself was not the point.

Other kinds of code and writing are different. Notes meant to be returned to are different. The product is the thing itself: the actual sentences, in the actual order, with the actual word choices. Every decision carries weight because the decisions are inseparable from the value. You cannot generate that and then love it the same way. The love is in the deciding.

This is why the eReader felt like such a useful filter. The things that earn a place on it are the things I or others have actually shaped. The things where the care is visible in the sentence itself.

Contrast this a little with a [Gist](https://gist.github.com/karpathy/442a6bf555914893e9891c11519de94f) Andrej Karpathy recently posted where the LLM is itself maintaining these [personal wiki articles](https://medium.com/@geetduggal/want-extraordinarily-useful-and-timeless-notes-build-a-personal-knowledge-encyclopedia-bc8b7fb09829) / main documents for notable folders.

> You never (or rarely) write the wiki yourself — the LLM writes and maintains all of it. You’re in charge of sourcing, exploration, and asking the right questions.

My view is slightly different. I think the LLM should help considerably with capture or collection and as a companion to suggest angles for how specifically to think and write about a topic, but the decisions about what matters amongst all that content should be up to you and ideally encoded in the sentences *you* write. In this sense the writing exists to most purely represent *your* thinking.

AI is therefore useful, and increasingly so. It captures faster than I can. It organizes faster than I can. It can produce code and writing for almost anything an order of magnitude faster than me.

The existence of this capability changes how we decide what’s worth keeping. There are endless more ways to generate relevant information for you and the bar for what deserves attention has to rise or else you might drown in noise.

This doesn’t mean everything you produce and consume has to be brilliant. (That is its own trap.) But it does mean you should *care* about the stuff you choose to live with, return to, and put in front of yourself. That’s your job.

It’s worth noting that raising of the bar improves AI too. The more we demand of ourselves, the more we ask of the tools, the more interesting the loop between human and machine becomes. The mediocre layer compresses. The signal surfaces, and both sides get sharper.

The X4 did not change my workflow because it has special powers. It does not. It is a small, cheap, single-purpose reading device with thoughtful firmware and just enough friction to be useful.

What it did for me was make a question concrete that had been floating abstractly for a long time. What deserves to live in the most considered, undistracted corner of my attention? The answer turned out to be sharper than I expected. Not bullet point summaries. Not aggregations. Not AI-generated reflections of my own thinking. Just prose I had shaped myself, or work by others that earned the same standard.

This is the kind of clarity I didn’t even sign up for when getting the device. And it came in a package that fits in a pocket and costs less than a dinner out.

So, if the noise has been rising for you too, and you have been wondering what to do about it, try this. Put a small dedicated reading device in your pocket. Then, every time you go to load something onto it, ask yourself whether it actually belongs. You might find that the device is not the magic. The question is. ‘Till next time 🏔️
