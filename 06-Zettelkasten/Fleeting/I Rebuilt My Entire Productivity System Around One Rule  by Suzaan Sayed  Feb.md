# I Rebuilt My Entire Productivity System Around One Rule.

Press enter or click to view image in full size

../_resources/1_K-LL8R1JWAv7QPJPSUQkRg_b3109391686346e4addf05b03.png

A developer I follow tweeted his entire productivity system:

*“If I’m not scared, it’s not real work.”*

That’s it. No elaborate framework. No apps. No methods.

One sentence. One filter. One rule.

Someone replied: “But what about planning? Organization? Tracking?”

His response: *“If planning scares me, I plan. If it doesn’t, I skip it. Same rule applies to everything.”*

He’s shipped 12 projects in 18 months. Most developers I know are still planning their first.

**One rule beats a hundred tactics.**

## The Search for the Perfect System

This week, I’ve shown you the traps:

63% of work is fake (). We watch instead of create (). We organize instead of execute (). We pursue 20 goals and achieve none ().

Each day, the same question in comments: *“Okay, but what DO I do instead?”*

Fair question. Here’s the uncomfortable answer:

**You don’t need a new system. You need one principle.**

I’ve studied high-output creators for months. Asked when I could: “What’s your productivity system?”

Expected: detailed processes, specific tools, elaborate frameworks.

Got: shockingly simple one-liners.

Not because they’re simplistic. Because they’ve compressed years of experimentation into a single guiding rule.

## The One-Rule Systems That Actually Work

Here are real examples from people who ship consistently:

**Writer (80K followers, daily publisher):** *“If I can’t explain the value in one sentence, I don’t write it.”*

No content calendars. No elaborate planning. Just: can I justify this in one sentence? Yes = write. No = skip.

**Designer (6-figure freelancer):** *“If the client can’t tell me exactly what they want in 5 minutes, it’s not ready to be designed.”*

No endless revision cycles. No scope creep. Client clarity = project starts. Client confusion = project waits.

**Entrepreneur (3 profitable businesses):** *“If it doesn’t make money or teach me something, I don’t do it.”*

No “networking for the sake of it.” No “visibility plays.” Every action: revenue or education. Nothing else.

**Developer (ships weekly):** *“If I can’t build an ugly version in a week, the idea is too complex.”*

No perfect architectures. No over-engineering. If it can’t be rough-but-working in 7 days, simplify or abandon.

**Creator (200K YouTube):** *“If my past self from a year ago wouldn’t watch this, I don’t make it.”*

No chasing trends. No algorithm gaming. Would year-ago me find value? Yes = create. No = skip.

**The pattern: Complex experience distilled into simple criteria.**

## Why One Rule Beats Complex Systems

A productivity researcher shared something that stuck:

*“Every decision you make depletes your willpower. Elaborate systems require hundreds of micro-decisions daily. One rule requires one decision: does this fit my rule?”*

**Example breakdown:**

**Complex system decisions:**

- Which project category does this fit?

- What’s the priority level?

- When should I schedule this?

- Which tool should I use?

- How should I tag this?

- What’s the status?

- Who should I assign this to?

- What are the dependencies?

**8 decisions before you even start working.**

**One-rule system decisions:**

- Does this fit my rule?

**1 decision. Then work.**

A founder said: *“I spent more mental energy managing my task system than doing tasks. One rule eliminated decision fatigue. Now I just work.”*

## How to Find Your One Rule

The rule isn’t arbitrary. It emerges from understanding what actually drives your output.

Here’s the pattern I’ve seen work:

**Step 1: Track what you actually do for 2 weeks** Not what you plan to do. What you actually complete.

**Step 2: Identify the pattern** What do your completed tasks have common? What do incomplete tasks share?

**Step 3: Find the discriminator** What’s the ONE difference between tasks you finish and tasks you avoid?

**Step 4: Turn it into a question** Frame your discriminator as a yes/no question.

**Step 5: Test for 30 days** Apply the rule ruthlessly. Refine based on results.

## Real Examples of Rule Discovery

**Designer’s journey:**

Tracked 2 weeks. Noticed pattern:

- Completed: Projects with clear, specific client requests

- Abandoned: Projects with vague “make it pop” feedback

**Discriminator:** Client clarity

**Rule:** “If the client can’t tell me exactly what they want in 5 minutes, it’s not ready to be designed.”

**Result:** Stopped taking unclear projects. Income up 40%. Stress down 70%.

**Writer’s journey:**

Tracked 2 weeks. Noticed pattern:

- Published: Articles where value was immediately clear

- Drafted but never published: Articles that were “interesting but not sure why”

**Discriminator:** Clear value proposition

**Rule:** “If I can’t explain the value in one sentence, I don’t write it.”

**Result:** Published 3x more. Reader engagement up. Less second-guessing.

**Developer’s journey:**

Tracked 2 weeks. Noticed pattern:

- Shipped: Features built quickly with iteration

- Stuck: Features over-planned with “perfect” architecture

**Discriminator:** Speed to working prototype

**Rule:** “If I can’t build an ugly version in a week, the idea is too complex.”

**Result:** Ships 2–3 features monthly instead of planning 1 feature endlessly.

## The Most Common One-Rule Patterns

After observing successful creators across platforms, rules tend to cluster into categories:

**Fear-based (If scared = real work):**

- “If I’m not scared, it’s not real work”

- “If it feels too easy, I’m avoiding something harder”

- “If I’m comfortable, I’m not growing”

**Value-based (Does this create value?):**

- “If it doesn’t make money or teach me, I don’t do it”

- “If I can’t explain the value, I don’t do it”

- “If nobody would pay for this, why am I doing it for free?”

**Time-based (How long to result?):**

- “If I can’t finish it this week, it’s too big”

- “If planning takes longer than doing, just do it”

- “If it takes more than 2 minutes to organize, just execute”

**Clarity-based (Is this well-defined?):**

- “If I can’t write the outcome in one sentence, it’s not ready”

- “If the request isn’t specific, I say no”

- “If I don’t know what ‘done’ looks like, I don’t start”

**Energy-based (Does this energize me?):**

- “If I’m dreading it but it matters, do it first”

- “If I’m excited, it’s either brilliant or distraction — wait 24 hours to decide”

- “If I’m procrastinating, the task is poorly defined”

**Your rule will likely fit one of these patterns.**

## What Happens When You Apply One Rule

A creator who adopted the “If I’m not scared, it’s not real work” rule shared his first week:

**Monday:** Planned to reorganize files. Asked: Am I scared? No. Skipped it. Wrote article instead. Scared? Yes. Published.

**Tuesday:** Wanted to research competitors. Asked: Am I scared? No. It’s comfortable. Skipped. Created product demo. Scared? Yes. Shipped.

**Wednesday:** Scheduled 3 “networking” calls. Asked: Am I scared? No. These feel easy. Realized they were procrastination. Cancelled. Built landing page. Scared? Yes. Launched.

**Thursday:** Started another tutorial course. Asked: Am I scared? No. I’m hiding. Stopped course. Published vulnerable post about failures. Scared? Terrified. Hit publish.

**Friday:** Weekly review of week. Output: 1 article, 1 demo, 1 landing page, 1 vulnerable post. Previous week with no rule: 0 output, lots of “busyness.”

He told me: *“The rule exposed how much of my ‘work’ was just sophisticated avoidance. If it didn’t scare me, it wasn’t moving me forward.”*

## The Resistance to One Rule

When I share this concept, I get pushback:

*“But what about tasks that HAVE to be done but aren’t scary? Like taxes?”*

Great question. Here’s what rule-followers do:

**They have a secondary rule for maintenance work.**

Example: The “scared = real work” person also has: *“Maintenance work: batch monthly, minimum time possible.”*

Primary rule = creation and growth Secondary rule = necessary admin

**The key: maintenance gets batched and minimized, not optimized.**

Taxes? Do them once, quickly, move on. Don’t build elaborate systems around them.

Email? Check twice daily, respond fast, done. Don’t optimize inbox zero.

**One rule for what matters. Minimal system for what doesn’t.**

## How to Test Your Rule

Found a potential rule? Test it for 7 days.

**Daily check:**

1. Morning: Write your rule on paper

2. Before each task: Ask if it fits the rule

3. If yes: Do it

4. If no: Skip it (or archive for later)

5. Evening: Count tasks done vs tasks skipped

**What to track:**

- How many tasks fit your rule?

- How many didn’t?

- What got accomplished?

- What got avoided?

- Did output increase?

**After 7 days:**

- If output up + stress down = keep the rule

- If output flat + confusion high = refine the rule

- If output down = wrong rule, try different angle

A developer tested 4 different rules before finding his. Each rule revealed something about his work patterns.

## What My Rule Became

After tracking my own patterns, my rule emerged:

**“If it doesn’t create something permanent, it’s not real work.”**

Applies to everything:

**Writing:** Creates permanent article → Real work **Planning:** Creates no permanent artifact → Skip (or 10 min max) **Email:** Creates no permanent value → Batch to 2x daily, 15 min each **Meetings:** Create no permanent artifact → Decline unless critical **Research:** Creates no permanent output → Only if immediately applied **Learning:** Creates no permanent skill → Only if I build something with it

**Result:**

**Before rule:** 40 hours “working,” ~12 hours creating permanent value **After rule:** 25 hours total, ~20 hours creating permanent value

**Less time, more output, higher quality.**

## The Freedom of Constraints

Sounds limiting, right? One rule?

It’s the opposite.

**Without a rule:** Every task is a negotiation. Should I do this? Maybe? What if? But…

**With a rule:** Every task is binary. Fits rule? Do it. Doesn’t fit? Skip it.

**Decisions drop from hundreds to dozens.**

A creator said: *“People think one rule is restrictive. It’s liberating. I stopped debating with myself 50 times daily. The rule decides. I execute.”*

## Common Mistakes When Adopting One Rule

**Mistake 1: Making the rule too complex**

Bad: “If it aligns with my quarterly objectives, supports my long-term vision, and provides value to my target audience while fitting my content calendar…”

Good: “If it scares me, it’s real work.”

**Keep it to one sentence.**

**Mistake 2: Having exceptions immediately**

“My rule is X, but sometimes Y, except when Z, unless…”

If your rule has exceptions, it’s not a rule. It’s a guideline.

**Rules are binary. No exceptions for 30 days minimum.**

**Mistake 3: Changing the rule too fast**

Tested for 3 days, doesn’t feel perfect, abandons it.

**Give every rule 30 days before evaluating.**

**Mistake 4: Not writing it down**

Kept the rule “in mind.” Forgot it by Tuesday.

**Write your rule somewhere you see it daily.**

Morning coffee spot. Laptop wallpaper. Phone lock screen.

**Mistake 5: Adopting someone else’s rule without testing**

Saw “If I’m scared, it’s real work” and adopted it without understanding if fear indicates growth for you.

**Your rule must match YOUR patterns, not someone else’s.**

## What This Means for Your Current System

If you currently have:

- Multiple apps

- Elaborate workflows

- Complex organizational structures

- Detailed processes

**You don’t need to delete everything immediately.**

Start here:

**Week 1:** Develop your rule (track patterns) **Week 2:** Test your rule alongside current system **Week 3:** Notice what the rule makes obsolete **Week 4:** Remove what you’re not using

Most people find their elaborate system naturally collapses.

Not because they forced it. Because the rule made it unnecessary.

A founder told me: *“I didn’t delete my task manager. I just stopped opening it. The rule handled everything. After 3 weeks, I realized I hadn’t used the app. Deleted it. Nothing broke.”*

## The Ultimate Simplification

Five days ago, I started this series showing you what’s broken:

Fake work. Consumption instead of creation. Organization instead of execution. Too many goals.

**Today’s the solution: One rule that cuts through all of it.**

Because every productivity problem stems from the same source: **too many decisions about what to do.**

One rule eliminates 90% of decisions.

What’s left? Work.

A creator summarized it perfectly: *“Productivity systems exist to help you decide what to do. One rule decides for you. Then you just do it. Turns out, that’s all productivity ever was.”*
