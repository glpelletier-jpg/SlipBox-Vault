# Longevity is stronger evidence of future value than novelty

> **One idea. One note.** Write for your future self — complete enough to understand without context.

---

## The idea

For non-perishable things — ideas, technologies, habits, books, tools — the longer something has already survived, the longer it is likely to continue surviving. Nassim Taleb named this the Lindy Effect: every year of survival is evidence of robustness, so a 500-year-old practice has earned more trust than a 5-year-old one, not less.

The key distinction is *non-perishable*. This applies to ideas, technologies, and cultural artifacts — not to biological organisms, which age in the conventional direction. The spoon that has existed for 10,000 years is more likely to exist in 10,000 more years than the app that launched last year.

---

## Why it matters

It inverts a common bias: we treat novelty as a signal of quality. The Lindy Effect says novelty is actually a risk factor — most new things fail quickly, and the ones that survive the early period have demonstrated something. What has survived already has the advantage.

Practical applications:
- **Information diet**: A book written in 1985 that still circulates has passed a harsh filter. A blog post from last week has not.
- **Tools and systems**: A well-worn methodology (GTD, Zettelkasten, double-entry bookkeeping) earns more confidence than a new productivity app.
- **Learning what to study**: The mathematics that has been useful for 200 years will be useful for 200 more. The specific API you learned last year may not.

---

## Evidence / examples

Nassim Nicholas Taleb (*Antifragile*): The life expectancy of a non-perishable technology is proportional to its current age. Every additional period of survival implies longer remaining life expectancy — mortality rate *decreases* with time.

Personal heuristic: *let the cream rise to the top before you take a sip* — wait for TV series, books, and tools to earn sustained reputation before investing time in them.
