# Small consistent actions compound into disproportionate outcomes

> **One idea. One note.** Write for your future self — complete enough to understand without context.

---

## The idea

Compounding is not just a financial principle. It applies to any domain where output at time T becomes input at time T+1: knowledge, skill, fitness, reputation, relationships, writing. A 1% daily improvement for one year produces a 37x outcome, not a 365% improvement — the returns are exponential, not linear, because each gain builds on the accumulated base.

The implication is counterintuitive: in compounding systems, *consistency* over time dominates *intensity* in the short term. One focused hour per day for five years will outperform five-hour sprints done inconsistently, not because of discipline but because of math.

---

## Why it matters

This reframes the standard productivity question. Instead of "how can I be more productive today?" the better question is "what practice, if done consistently for two years, would produce a disproportionate result?" That question has a smaller, more specific answer.

It also explains why beginners feel slow: they're in the early part of the curve where compounding hasn't had time to work. The experienced practitioner appears to learn faster because their accumulated base is larger — each new thing connects to more existing things.

---

## Evidence / examples

Richard Hamming, as reported by colleague Bode: *"Knowledge and productivity are like compound interest. The more you know, the more you learn; the more you learn, the more you can do... Given two people with exactly the same ability, the one person who manages day in and day out to get in one more hour of thinking will be tremendously more productive over a lifetime."*

James Clear (*Atomic Habits*): The 1% rule — 1.01^365 = 37.78. The same logic applied to habits.

Personal application: The Zettelkasten itself is a compounding system. The first 50 permanent notes feel like slow work. The 500th note has 499 potential connections it didn't have before.
