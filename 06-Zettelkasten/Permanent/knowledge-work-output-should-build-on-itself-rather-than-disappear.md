# Knowledge work output should build on itself rather than disappear

> **One idea. One note.** Write for your future self — complete enough to understand without context.

---

## The idea

Most knowledge work is ephemeral: you write a careful email, it lands in your sent folder and subtly fades from both minds. You take notes on a book, close the notebook, and the notes age in isolation. The effort was real; the output evaporated.

A system designed for compounding works differently: every output is built *on top of* prior outputs rather than beside them. The Zettelkasten is the canonical example — each new permanent note is placed in relation to existing notes, so the structure of knowledge grows denser over time rather than wider and flatter.

Richard Hamming described this as compound interest on thinking: *"The more you know, the more you learn; the more you learn, the more you can do; the more you can do, the more the opportunity. Given two people of exactly the same ability, the one person who manages day in and day out to get in one more hour of thinking will be tremendously more productive over a lifetime."*

---

## Why it matters

The practical test: could you write a serious essay on something you studied 18 months ago without re-reading? If not, the work didn't accrete — it was consumed. A system with genuine accumulation means the answer is yes, because the processed ideas are still there in a form you can build on.

This is why a Zettelkasten with 200 dense permanent notes is more useful than a folder of 2,000 captured articles: one is a structure, the other is a pile.

---

## Evidence / examples

Sönke Ahrens (*How to Take Smart Notes*): *"Without a permanent reservoir of ideas, you will not be able to develop any major ideas over a longer period of time because you are restricting yourself either to the length of a single project or the capacity of your memory."*

Anna Gát on Twitter: *"On Twitter, you don't build anything."* — a neat compression of the same idea applied to social media.
