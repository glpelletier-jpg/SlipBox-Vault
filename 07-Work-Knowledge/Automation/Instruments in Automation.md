## Chapter 1

### Calculus

- Calculus is described as a powerful tool in mathematics for analyzing changes in the real world. The author emphasizes a pragmatic approach to its principles for those engaged in industrial process measurement and control.

#### 1.1 Introduction to calculus

- The author introduces calculus as the study of changes and highlights its complexities for learners. The use of delta (∆) signifies changes, while differentials (d) represent infinitesimal changes.

- The chapter explains how to express changes mathematically and sets up the basic notions of differentiation and integration, providing the groundwork for understanding instrumentation.

#### 1.2 The concept of differentiation

- Differentiation enables the measurement of rates of change. The example given details how to calculate the flow rate of propane indirectly through mass measurements over time.

- The instantaneous flow rate is defined as the ratio of mass (dm) and time (dt), showcasing practical applications in instrumentation. The derivation of derivatives is discussed, connecting it to physical concepts like velocity and acceleration.

#### 1.3 The concept of integration

- Integration is presented as the reverse process of differentiation, used to calculate total quantities such as mass over time from mass flow rates.

- Summation of flow rates over time is highlighted, and the author explains how to derive mass lost from flow rates through multiplication of average flow over time periods, illustrating the geometric area under a curve as a representation of total quantity.

#### 1.4 How derivatives and integrals relate to one another

- The relationship between derivatives and integrals is discussed as inverse processes. The author shows how differentiation converts mass into flow rate, while integration infers mass from flow rate.

- This duality between the two concepts is reinforced through real-life applications, further stressed by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

#### 1.5 Symbolic versus numerical calculus

- The text contrasts symbolic calculus, which manipulates mathematical symbols, with numerical calculus, which approximates derivatives and integrals using data.

- Numerical differentiation and integration techniques are emphasized as more accessible for understanding real-world data without needing complex mathematical expressions.

#### 1.6 Numerical differentiation

- Practical applications of numerical differentiation are explored, particularly in processes like pipeline pressure monitoring. The author explains how to effectively calculate rates of change using recorded measurements and a computer.

- Real-world examples, including pressure measurements and trends, demonstrate how numerical differentiation applies to instrumentation for diagnosing issues like pipeline ruptures.

### Calculus

#### 1.6 Numerical Differentiation

- Numerical differentiation is the method of estimating the rate of change of a quantity by observing data at discrete intervals. This technique is particularly useful in instrumentation to calculate the rate of pressure change over time.

- For example, using pressure data, the pressure change rate (in PSI/minute) can be calculated by subtracting two adjacent pressure readings and dividing by the time interval, revealing significant changes like pipeline ruptures by detecting sudden drops in pressure.

### 1.7 Numerical Integration

- Numerical integration calculates accumulated quantities based on a measured flow rate of fluids over time. The fundamental concept embodies multiplying a variable by small increments of another variable.

#### 1.7.1 Accumulating fluid volumes

- The integration of flow rates over time yields the total volume of fluid passing through a pipe. This is expressed mathematically as the integral of flow flow rate (Q) over a certain time interval.

#### 1.7.2 Totalizers in flow measurement

- Flow integrators, or totalizers, are physical devices that perform integration operations by accumulating fluid volumes. For example, an odometer-like display in a flow totalizer calculates and shows the total mass or volume passed through a system.

#### 1.7.3 Example: Utilizing flow integrators

- Utilizing turbines in pneumatic systems demonstrates how flow totalizers can track variations in fluid flow. Variations in turbine speed correlate directly to flow rates, aiding in accurate fluid measurement and control.
