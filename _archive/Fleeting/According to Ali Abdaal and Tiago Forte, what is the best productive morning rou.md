# According to Ali Abdaal and Tiago Forte, what is the best productive morning routine

Here's a synthesis of what Ali Abdaal and Tiago Forte each recommend for a productive morning, drawn from their actual writing and content:

---

## Ali Abdaal's Morning Approach

Ali's philosophy is rooted in his book *Feel-Good Productivity* — the morning should set an energizing, not anxious, tone.

**1. Separate planning from doing ("The Pilot and the Plane")**
First thing in the morning, Ali acts as "The Pilot" — he grabs a morning coffee, takes a step back, and figures out what he wants to get done that day. Then he switches into "The Plane" mode, where his only job is to execute the orders The Pilot already gave. Crucially, he doesn't need to worry about planning or think too hard about what he could be doing.

**2. Set one Daily Highlight**
He chooses just one thing he's going to do that day and fully commits to it — maybe calling someone, scripting a video, or going for a run. The point is choosing just one thing as the priority. He recommends starting it with a verb for clarity (e.g., "Write Sunday's newsletter" rather than just "newsletter") and being specific enough that it can be done in a single sitting.

**3. Time-block that highlight**
Once he's set his Daily Highlight, he time-blocks it in his calendar and protects that time from meetings or distractions — giving his future "Plane" self the clearest possible directions.

**4. Make a "Might-Do" list**
The final (optional) step is a brain dump of tasks he *might* do if he feels like it. This clears mental clutter and confirms the Daily Highlight is the highest priority. If he finds himself wondering "isn't there something more important I should be doing?", he can trust The Pilot has everything under control.

**5. Energizing physical habits**
Abdaal suggests beginning your day with exercises that stimulate you — like physical activity, meditation, or reading — to boost productivity and mental clarity. His broader philosophy: the secret to productivity isn't grind, it's feeling good. If you're working toward somewhere you genuinely want to be, motivation and discipline become much easier.

---

## Tiago Forte's Morning Approach

Tiago's work focuses less on a fixed morning ritual and more on *systems* that make mornings frictionless. His key contributions:

**1. Do your planning the night before (or first thing)**
He advises choosing your quietest time with uninterrupted focus — either waking up earlier than everyone else or spending a few minutes at the end of the previous day to identify the tasks you *must* do the next day. Making this a non-negotiable time every day turns it into a habit.

**2. Process your inboxes in order (the "ECNT" system)**
Tiago follows a four-step sequence: first Email, then Calendar (turning any meetings or deadlines into tasks so his calendar and to-do list stay aligned), then Notes (reviewing his note-taking app for ideas or project notes that need action), and finally Tasks (pulling everything together in his task manager and prioritizing the day and week by what's urgent vs. what can wait).

**3. Dedicate mornings to deep work**
Tiago recommends structuring the day in focused blocks — for example, morning for deep work and afternoon for shallow tasks — dedicating peak hours to cognitively demanding work to optimize effectiveness while replenishing creative reserves.

**4. Use the Weekly Review as the backbone**
Rather than reinventing each morning, Tiago's system anchors the week. Once a Weekly Review is established, it can serve as a platform for any other habits or routines you'd like to stick to — reviewing goals, setting an intention for the week, or planning ahead.

---

## Where They Agree

Both converge on a few core ideas:

- **Plan before you do** — don't start the day reacting

- **Identify your one most important task** — don't treat everything as equal

- **Protect morning hours for deep, focused work**

- **Keep the routine short and sustainable** — design a simple morning routine, thirty minutes or less, with two or three habits you'd like to start your day with

The biggest difference: Ali leans into the *feel-good* emotional side of mornings (energy, enjoyment, play), while Tiago leans into the *systems* side (inboxes, capture, reviews). A combined routine drawing on both would be quite powerful.



# Claude generated Morning Routine Script

Here's a morning routine script synthesizing both Ali Abdaal's and Tiago Forte's frameworks:

---

# Your Productive Morning Routine

### (~45–60 minutes total)

---

## ☀️ Wake Up + Body First (10 min)

*Get out of bed immediately — no phone yet.*

Do one energizing physical act: a short walk, some stretching, or a quick workout. The goal isn't fitness — it's signaling to your brain that the day has started. Drink a glass of water. Make your coffee or tea.

---

## 🧘 Mind Clearance (5 min)

*Still no phone or email.*

Sit quietly for 5 minutes. This can be meditation, deep breathing, or simply sitting with your coffee in silence. Ali calls this protecting your mental state before the world gets to it. You're warming up the Pilot before takeoff.

---

## ✈️ Pilot Mode: Daily Planning (15 min)

*Now you can open your tools — but only for planning, not reacting.*

Work through Tiago's ECNT sequence quickly:

1. **Email** — scan for anything truly urgent. Don't reply. Just flag.

2. **Calendar** — look at today's schedule. Turn any meetings or deadlines into concrete tasks.

3. **Notes** — glance at yesterday's captures. Anything that needs action today? Add it to your task list.

4. **Tasks** — open your task manager. Survey everything on your plate.

Now, following Ali's system, **choose your one Daily Highlight** — the single most important thing you will get done today. Write it as a verb phrase: *"Draft the proposal introduction"* not *"proposal."* Then time-block it on your calendar and protect that slot.

Finally, do a quick **Might-Do brain dump** — list everything else you *could* do today, without pressure. This offloads mental clutter and confirms your Highlight is the right priority.

---

## 🛫 Switch to Plane Mode: Deep Work Begins (remaining morning)

*Planning is over. Execution begins.*

Close email. Put your phone on Do Not Disturb. Your only job now is to work on your Daily Highlight. No decisions, no replanning — The Pilot has already handled that. Trust the system and fly.

---

## 🔁 The Weekly Anchor (Monday mornings only, +20 min)

On Monday mornings, add Tiago's Weekly Review before Pilot Mode:

- What did you accomplish last week?

- What's on the calendar this week?

- What's your Highlight for the whole week — not just today?

- Are your notes and inboxes cleared?

This transforms your Monday morning into a control tower briefing for the entire week.

---

## A Few Ground Rules

- **No social media or news until after deep work.** Both Ali and Tiago agree: reactive inputs pollute your planning mind.

- **Keep the routine short.** If it takes longer than 60 minutes before you're working, trim it. Sustainability beats perfection.

- **Do an energy audit monthly.** Ali recommends reviewing which parts of your routine actually energize you vs. drain you, and adjusting accordingly.

---

The core idea behind this whole routine, in one sentence: ***plan like a Pilot in the morning, execute like a Plane for the rest of the day, and trust your Second Brain to catch everything else.***



# For morning routines in general, accessing the entirety of the information you have, what needs to be added or adjusted

Great question. Here's what's missing or worth adjusting based on broader research on morning routines:

---

## What to Add

**1. Don't skip the night before**
The best morning routines are actually set up the evening before. Laying out clothes, prepping your bag, and doing a light brain dump before bed dramatically reduces morning friction and decision fatigue. Your morning routine effectively starts at 10pm.

**2. Delay caffeine by 90 minutes**
Neuroscientist Andrew Huberman's research suggests waiting 90 minutes after waking before your first coffee. Cortisol naturally peaks in that window and does the alertness work caffeine would otherwise do. Drinking coffee immediately blunts that natural spike and leads to an earlier afternoon crash.

**3. Get morning sunlight within the first 30 minutes**
This is one of the most evidence-backed habits available. Even 5–10 minutes of outdoor light exposure (or near a bright window) sets your circadian rhythm, boosts serotonin, and sharpens alertness far more than any app or productivity hack. It's free and takes almost no time.

**4. No-input window**
The current script says "no phone" but doesn't explain *why* strongly enough. Your brain is in a highly suggestible, creative, theta-wave state for roughly 20–30 minutes after waking. Filling that window with news, social media, or notifications hijacks your attentional system before you've had a chance to set your own agenda. Guard it deliberately.

**5. Cold exposure or temperature shift**
A cold shower or even ending a warm shower with 30–60 seconds of cold water triggers a significant norepinephrine and dopamine release — not as a productivity gimmick but as a genuine neurochemical reset. Wim Hof research and Huberman's work both support this as one of the highest-ROI biological levers available in the morning.

**6. Intentional first input**
Once you do allow inputs, be deliberate about what the *first* thing you read or listen to is. Many high performers (Tim Ferriss, Ryan Holiday) read something philosophical, inspirational, or from a physical book before touching work tools. This primes your thinking quality before the tactical planning begins.

**7. A genuine transition ritual**
There should be a clear, physical signal that marks the shift from "morning routine" to "work mode" — making a specific drink, putting on headphones, closing a door, lighting a candle. James Clear and Cal Newport both emphasize this: the brain responds to environmental cues, and a consistent transition ritual conditions deep focus faster over time.

---

## What to Adjust

**The planning block is slightly too reactive**
Starting Tiago's ECNT sequence with *email* — even just scanning — still anchors your morning to other people's priorities. A stronger version: start with your tasks and calendar first, then check email only to catch genuine fires. Flip the order to CNTE.

**The 45–60 minute estimate may be optimistic**
For most people just building this routine, 45–60 minutes is tight. A more honest framing is 60–90 minutes until the routine becomes habitual and automatic. Budget accordingly rather than starting the day already behind.

**"One Daily Highlight" needs a backup rule**
Ali's Daily Highlight concept is excellent but the script doesn't address what to do when your Highlight gets derailed by something urgent (which will happen). A simple rule to add: if your Highlight gets knocked out, immediately choose a new one rather than letting the day dissolve into reactive tasks.

**Movement needs more weight**
The current script treats physical movement as optional and light. Research increasingly shows that even 10–20 minutes of moderate cardio in the morning meaningfully improves executive function, working memory, and mood for 2–4 hours afterward. It's arguably more cognitively valuable than the planning block itself and deserves a firmer place in the routine.

---

## The Upgraded Core Principle

The original script's principle was good. An expanded version would be:

> *Own your biology first — light, movement, temperature. Then own your attention. Then plan. Then execute. Never let the outside world set the agenda before you've set your own.*



# Can you create a end of work day shut down routine

The routine is structured in three phases totaling about 20 minutes:

**Clear the decks** gets your email and browser tabs to zero so nothing bleeds into the evening. **Plan tomorrow** is the core of your routine — using Todoist to process tasks and picking your top 3 means you start tomorrow with direction, not friction. The calendar scan catches any surprises before they ambush you in the morning.

**Mental close** is the decompression layer. The win + open loop combo is based on Cal Newport's idea that you need a clear "your work is done" signal for your brain to actually stop working. Writing the open loop is especially powerful — it tells your nervous system the thought is safe to park until tomorrow.
