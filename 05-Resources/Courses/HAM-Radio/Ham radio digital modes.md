## Ham radio digital modes

Ham radio digital modes are **computer-controlled methods to send text, data, images, and even voice using radio waves**, enabling weak-signal communication and efficient data exchange, with popular examples including **FT8**, **PSK31**, **RTTY**, **JS8Call**, **SSTV**, and digital voice modes like **DMR**, using software (like WSJT-X or FLDIGI) to turn data into audio tones for transmission over standard radios, creating long-distance contacts where voice can't. Common Digital Modes

- **FT8/FT4:** Extremely popular for weak-signal, long-distance (DX) contacts, exchanging call signs and signal reports efficiently, often with modest equipment.

- **PSK31 (Phase Shift Keying):** Great for casual, keyboard-to-keyboard chats (ragchewing) over HF, requiring narrow bandwidth.

- **JS8Call:** A text-based mode for keyboard messaging, similar to PSK31 but optimized for weak signals and asynchronous communication.

- **RTTY (Radio Teletype):** A classic mode, popular in contesting, that sends text using frequency shifts.

- **SSTV (Slow Scan Television):** Transmits still images, allowing hams to send pictures over the air.

- **Winlink/Pactor/VAR:** Used for email and file transfer, offering store-and-forward messaging,.

- **DMR/D-Star/System Fusion:** Digital voice modes that use specialized radios for clear audio and data,.

- **APRS (Automatic Packet Reporting System):** Sends location data, weather info, and messages, often seen on VHF/UHF. How They Work

1. **Computer & Software:** A PC runs software (like WSJT-X or FLDIGI) to generate the digital signals.

2. **Interface:** A sound card or interface (like a DigiRig) connects the computer to your radio's microphone/speaker jacks.

3. **Audio Tones:** The software converts data (text, reports) into tones that are fed into the radio as audio.

4. **Radio Transmission:** The radio transmits these tones via SSB, FM, or other modulations.

5. **Decoding:** The receiving station uses similar software to decode the tones back into usable data. Why Use Them?

- **Weak Signal Performance:** Modes like FT8 can make contacts in conditions where voice or Morse code (CW) would fail.

- **Efficiency:** Send data, messages, or signal reports quickly and accurately.

- **Overcoming Limitations:** Digital processing can detect signals too faint for the human ear.
